Chapter 1: Global Windstorm

  • Q: why does the atmosphere of venus rotate at a high speed even though the underlying planet is almost stationary?

    • A: this phenomenon is called super-rotation. it’s complicated and not well understood. read this recent paper about it.

  • Q: earth’s rotational period is shorter than that of the moon. this causes the moon to be pushed away over time (this is a different effect than tidal braking). why?

    • A: See stackexchange. this has interesting implications if the satellite’s rotation is the opposite direction of its orbit (eg: venus). but for the common solar case, any rotation faster than the orbital period will transfer angular momentum to the other body.

  • general idea: flywheels. even if earth rotation stops, moon will restart it.

Chapter 3: Spent Fuel Pool

when treading water, I assumed that a side-to-side hand motion would just waste energy (it’s not directing water down for thrust). turns out this isn’t true! see this study.

The two inferior techniques largely rely on pushing down against the water to move the body upward. This has two problems: water is too thin to provide much support, and even when the pushing works you get a lot of wasted up-and-down motion. The two better strokes, in contrast, involve lateral movements of the arms and legs: your cupped hand acts like an airplane wing or sailboat sail, generating lift forces perpendicular to the plane of motion. This is more efficient than pushing on the water, and produces less wasted vertical bobbing.

Chapter ??: Machine Gun Jetpack

  • gravity will not cause torque on an airborne object; rocket engine doesn’t matter whether it’s on top (helicopter style) or bottom. see pendulum rocket fallacy. but it does matter for a hot air balloon, because its thrust vector points up and not along the craft axis. think about it.
  • look at the rocket equation. derive it ourselves – why does everything reduce to a mass density?
  • q: gau 8 avenger fires 60 1-pound bullets per second. why is this ever useful as compared to shells, etc? what’s the use case for this kind of gun?
    • a: gov testing indicates it’s useful against ground targets (tanks etc). officially provided materials also indicate it’s useful for anti-air in close range (missiles have minimum range requirements). but empirically those kills don’t seem to happen. some ex-air force people believe it’s only because the fighter pilots like having guns (inaccurate, they shake the plane and damage craft). but the guns and ammo only account for 2.5% of F-35 payload, so they put it on anyways.
